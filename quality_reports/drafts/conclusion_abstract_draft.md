## Abstract

We decompose agricultural producer sentiment into four dimensions of environmental turbulence — Volatility, Uncertainty, Complexity, and Ambiguity (VUCA) — using individual-level microdata from the Purdue University/CME Group Ag Economy Barometer (October 2015 through December 2024). For each dimension, we construct dual measures combining structured survey indices with open-ended text responses, standardized against a 2016–2019 baseline. Construct validity is supported by strong face validity at major agricultural shocks, a robust negative correlation between our Uncertainty measure and the AEB ($r = -0.758$), and low cross-dimension correlations confirming that the four dimensions capture conceptually distinct aspects of turbulence. Granger causality tests against Creighton University Main Street Economy indicators reveal that Volatility predicts farmland price changes at lags of one to four months, and that Ambiguity leads farm loan volume by approximately two months. These results demonstrate that disaggregating sentiment into VUCA dimensions provides economically meaningful information beyond what a single composite index captures, with direct implications for the study of agricultural land markets, farm credit demand, and producer decision-making under uncertainty.

## 6. Conclusion

This paper develops and validates the first VUCA-based decomposition of agricultural producer sentiment, using nine years of individual-level microdata from the Purdue University/CME Group Ag Economy Barometer. Our four dimension scores — Volatility, Uncertainty, Complexity, and Ambiguity — are constructed from a combination of structured survey indices and open-ended text responses, standardized against a pre-shock baseline period, and validated against both internal AEB measures and external agricultural economic indicators.

Several conclusions emerge from our analysis. First, the VUCA dimensions are statistically and economically distinct: cross-dimension correlations are moderate, and each dimension exhibits different patterns of elevation across major agricultural shocks. Second, our measures carry forward-looking information not contained in the composite AEB index: Volatility Granger-causes farmland price changes, and Ambiguity leads farm credit demand, at economically meaningful lag lengths. Third, the 2021–2022 period stands out as an episode of exceptionally high Complexity — a multidimensional operating environment characterized by simultaneous commodity price surges, input cost inflation, and supply chain disruption — that would have been indistinguishable from a simple "high sentiment" reading in the composite AEB index alone.

These findings suggest several avenues for future research. The VUCA framework could be extended to longer panel surveys, quarterly Farm Business Survey data, or international agricultural sentiment indices to assess its generalizability across production systems and policy environments. The Granger causality results, particularly for Volatility and farmland prices, warrant further investigation using structural VAR methods and, where possible, hard administrative data on land transactions. The Ambiguity dimension, which exhibited the weakest measurement performance in the current study, may benefit from domain-adapted lexicons and longer text response formats that enable more reliable detection of mixed-signal environments.

More broadly, our results argue for moving beyond single-index characterizations of agricultural producer sentiment. When farm confidence declines, the policy-relevant question is not only "how much" but "why" — whether the deterioration reflects rapid change (Volatility), opaque futures (Uncertainty), simultaneous pressures (Complexity), or contradictory signals (Ambiguity). Each dimension has different implications for policy response, and our VUCA measures provide the empirical foundation for making these distinctions.
